历史概述

Historical Overview

Author

施耐庵

Shi Nai'an (c. 1296–1372), a Yuan-Ming dynasty writer. Some scholars also attribute parts to his student Luo Guanzhong.

Period

14世纪 · 明代

Written during the late Yuan or early Ming dynasty (c. 1368-1398), drawing from historical accounts of the Song dynasty rebellion.

Setting

北宋 · 梁山泊

The Northern Song dynasty (12th century), centered on Liangshan Marsh where 108 outlaws gather to fight corruption.

Scale

历史意义

Historical Significance

江湖义气与反抗精神

Water Margin is the first of the Chinese novels in vernacular, and it established the template for the "outlaw novel" genre. It tells the story of 108 outlaws who gather at Liangshan Marsh to form a rebel army, fighting against corrupt officials before eventually seeking amnesty from the Song government.

The novel's portrayal of loyalty (忠义, zhōngyì) among the heroes, combined with their resistance to unjust authority, has made it a symbol of righteous rebellion throughout Chinese history. It influenced secret societies, peasant uprisings, and revolutionary movements for centuries.

The young should not read Water Margin, and the old should not read Three Kingdoms.

— Traditional Chinese Proverb

东亚文学的深远影响

The novel's influence extends far beyond China. In Japan, it became "a ubiquitous presence in the literary and visual culture of early modern Japan," with a 1594 edition found in the library of a Tokugawa adviser. It profoundly influenced Japanese literature, manga, and anime, with adaptations like "Fist of the North Star" and "Saint Seiya" drawing directly from its characters.

The novel's 108 heroes inspired Konami's famous "Suikoden" video game series, which has sold millions of copies worldwide, demonstrating the story's enduring appeal across cultures and media.

为何经久不衰

Why It Endures

永恒的反抗主题

The novel's celebration of rebellion against corrupt authority resonates across time and cultures. Its 108 heroes—each with distinct personalities, skills, and backgrounds—represent a cross-section of Song society, from scholars and generals to fishermen and thieves.

The story's exploration of loyalty, brotherhood, and justice continues to inspire adaptations in film, television, literature, and gaming. Its characters have become cultural archetypes recognized throughout East Asia.

丰富的艺术遗产

Water Margin has been adapted into countless forms: Peking opera, martial arts films (including classics by Shaw Brothers), television series, manga, anime, and video games. The novel's vivid characters and dramatic episodes—like Wu Song fighting the tiger with bare hands—have become iconic scenes in Chinese popular culture.

The novel also influenced the development of the wuxia (martial arts) genre, establishing many of the themes and character types that would define Chinese action literature for centuries.
