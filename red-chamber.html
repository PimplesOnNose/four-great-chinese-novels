历史概述

Historical Overview

Author

曹雪芹

Cao Xueqin (c. 1715–1763), a Qing dynasty writer. He completed the first 80 chapters; the final 40 were added by Gao E and Cheng Weiyuan.

Period

18世纪 · 清代

Written during the mid-Qing dynasty (c. 1750s), reflecting the author's own family's rise and fall from imperial favor.

Setting

清代 · 大观园

The Qing dynasty, centered on the Jia family mansion and the beautiful Prospect Garden (大观园) in Beijing.

Scale

120回 · 百万余字

The longest of the four novels, with 120 chapters and over one million characters, featuring 447 named characters.

历史意义

Historical Significance

中国文学的巅峰之作

Dream of the Red Chamber is widely regarded as the greatest of the four classic Chinese novels. Scholar Wang Guowei called it "the tragedy of tragedies" and proclaimed it "worthy of being considered as the one great masterpiece in the realm of Chinese art." The novel has been compared to the greatest works of world literature, including Don Quixote and Proust's In Search of Lost Time.

The novel's semi-autobiographical nature—drawing from Cao Xueqin's own family's dramatic fall from imperial favor—gives it an emotional authenticity that transcends its fictional framework.

It is beyond question one of the great novels of all literature.

— Anthony West, The New Yorker

红学的诞生

The novel's influence gave rise to an entire scholarly field: Redology (红学, Hóngxué)—the study of Dream of the Red Chamber. This field has produced thousands of studies, exploring everything from the novel's textual history to its philosophical depths. Mao Zedong himself claimed to have read the novel five times and praised it as one of China's greatest works of literature.

The novel's exploration of gender, class, and the decline of aristocratic families has made it a rich subject for feminist, Marxist, and postmodern interpretations.

为何经久不衰

Why It Endures

永恒的人性探索

The novel's exploration of love, loss, and the transience of earthly pleasures resonates across time and cultures. The tragic romance between Jia Baoyu and Lin Daiyu—set against the decline of the Jia family—has become one of the most beloved love stories in world literature.

The novel's philosophical depth, blending Buddhist and Taoist concepts of illusion and reality with Confucian social critique, gives it layers of meaning that reveal new insights with each reading.

艺术成就与文化传承

The novel's unprecedented psychological depth, its encyclopedic depiction of Chinese material culture—from medicine and cuisine to opera and architecture—and its masterful use of poetry have established it as the pinnacle of Chinese literary achievement.

Its influence extends to modern Chinese literature, film, television, and theater. The 1987 television adaptation is considered a near-definitive version, and the novel continues to inspire new interpretations and adaptations in the 21st century.

主要人物

Key Characters

贾

贾宝玉

Jia Baoyu · The Jade-Like Prince

The novel's protagonist, a sensitive and poetic young man who rejects Confucian expectations in favor of love and beauty. His name means "precious jade."

林

林黛玉

Lin Daiyu · The Black Jade

A beautiful, intelligent, and deeply emotional young woman. Her tragic love for Baoyu and early death have made her an icon of romantic longing.

薛

薛宝钗

Xue Baochai · The Golden Lock

Baoyu's eventual wife, representing practical wisdom and Confucian virtue. Her contrast with Daiyu creates the novel's central romantic tension.

王

王熙凤

Wang Xifeng · The Phoenix

The brilliant and ruthless manager of the Jia household. Her strength and ambition challenge traditional gender roles in Qing society.
